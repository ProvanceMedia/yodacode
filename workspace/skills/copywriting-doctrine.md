# Copywriting Doctrine (House Voice)

## When to use
BEFORE drafting anything a human will read as prose: outreach letters, cold
emails, follow-ups, marketing copy, website text, or email drafts for the user's
signature. Not after — the doctrine shapes the draft, it doesn't decorate it.

## The gate (mandatory, before any draft is saved or shown)
Read the draft aloud, sentence by sentence. For each one: **would a human being
actually say this, out loud, to this specific reader?** Rewrite until yes.
Then the cut pass: delete the first sentence — if the piece still works, keep
the deletion and test the new first sentence the same way. Aim to cut ~30%.

## Ban the MOVE, not the phrase (synonyms are the same offence)
- **No throat-clearing openers** in any wording: "I wanted to reach out…",
  "Just checking in", "Quick one". The first sentence IS the reader's problem
  or the point.
- **No fake-folksy performance**: nothing that stages an action that didn't
  happen ("I wanted to sit down and write…" — nobody sat down).
- **No eager-assistant constructions**: "Happy to…", "Keen to…", "Would love
  to…" → say the plain version ("I can confirm the price once I have the specs").
- **No fake social proof as a segue** ("A few customers have also asked…" to
  smuggle in a second pitch).
- **One dash-reveal per piece, max.** "X — the thing you forgot" is a fine move
  once; three times is a tell. Vary sentence shapes.

## Structure rules
1. **Open with the reader's problem**, ideally borrowing credibility ("Our
   customers tell us the same thing: …") — warm, not clipped.
2. **One audience, one offer, one CTA per piece.** A second offer gets ONE
   line, its own paragraph, framed as a door not a pitch ("Just need X? That
   works too.").
3. **Name the reader's world with insider vocabulary** — the terms they use at
   work, not generic descriptors. Precision builds trust; adjectives don't.
4. **Plain signpost sentences anchor paragraphs** ("The process is simple.").
5. **CTA = soft question + concrete next step** ("Open to seeing how this works
   for your team? Reply and I'll walk you through getting started.").
6. **Proof points only from approved materials.** Never invent or embellish a
   stat, price, or claim.

## Register calibration
The target is "the user on a good day" — their warmth, their sign-off, their
level of salesmanship — NOT minimalist terseness. Warmth is in voice; what
kills a piece is incohesion: two audiences, trench-coat sentences, bullet
points wearing a letter costume. Cohesion is king: every paragraph hands off
to the next along ONE thread.

## Your voice anchors — ADD THESE (highest-leverage section)
Paste 2–3 pieces the user actually wrote and would happily send again (a cold
letter, a chase email, a customer reply). Examples beat rules: the rules stop
the worst moves, the anchors set the register. Keep them current — swap in
better ones as they appear.

## Steps
1. Load this skill BEFORE drafting. Identify: the one audience, the one offer,
   the one CTA. If you have two of any, split into two pieces.
2. Draft in the anchors' register (or, with no anchors yet, the structure rules
   above — and tell the user this skill works far better with real examples).
3. Run the gate: read aloud, cut the first sentence if it survives without it,
   cut ~30%, check the ban-the-move list.
4. For email drafts on an existing thread, ALSO read the user's own messages in
   that thread and match their greeting/sign-off/formality over the anchors.
